 The International Society for Presence Research (ISPR)is a non-profit membership organization founded in 2002 to support academic research related to the concept of (tele)presence. 
 Telepresence, often shortened to presence, is commonly referred to as a sense of ‘being there’ in a virtual environment and more broadly defined as an illusion of nonmediation in which users of any technology overlook or misconstrue the technology’s role in their experience (seehref="https://ispr.info/about-presence-2/about-presence/" - Presence defined
for more details). 
 This web site is intended to serve as a resource for those who conduct research, develop theory, design, market, write about, or simply are interested in, the concept and phenomena of presence. 
 We encourage your questions, contributions, comments and suggestions regarding ISPR and all aspects of this website; please feel free to contact us viahref="https://ispr.info/home/contact/" - our webform
(or send an e-mail tolombard@temple.edu).

 Antwerp, BelgiumCrisis After Crisis After …: What About the Third Sector? 
 Post-Conference Publication OpportunitiesFollowing the conference, authors are invited to revise their papers in light of conference feedback.Members can then submit their papers to be reviewed for selection for publication in theISTRConference Working Papers Seriesto be published after the conference on the ISTR website.All papers are reviewed by a small committee (not a blind review). Guidelines for publication are provided on the submission page.Deadline to Submit: September 30, 2024More Information Available HereConference Report Now Available 
  
 ISTR’s 16th International Conference in Antwerp marked 30 years since our first ISTR International Conference in Pecs, Hungary in 1994. The theme of that inaugural conference was Towards the Year 2000. Then, 265 participants from 51 countries participated in the first conference to focus on international research perspectives on the third sector. As noted in the conference report, “going international” for the society at the time marked a distinct shift away from the US-focus on “nonprofit” and “volunteering and philanthropy” to “civil society, mutuality, social economy, and community.” 
  
 30 years later, the 16th International conference hosted 600 participants from more than 65 countries, including a handful of scholars who had been present in 1994 and ten past presidents of the Society. We continue to engage with research on core aspects of the third sector, but our conference has evolved to reflect our members’ research interests. We notice increased interest in the boundaries of what is considered third sector action, in connecting research to practice, on climate and sustainability, uncivil society, and on research from critical and southern perspectives. We are energized by the diversity of this field and the conversations that our conferences foster!
